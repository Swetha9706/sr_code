Platform Overview

Between informal tools and enterprise complexity

Krētha is an ongoing B2B retail procurement platform designed to bring retailers, wholesalers, and manufacturers into one connected operational system — instead of leaving procurement scattered across calls, chats, spreadsheets, and memory.

At its core, the product responds to a practical business reality: small and mid-sized businesses often manage high-volume purchasing through fragmented workflows that were never built for visibility, accountability, or scale. Orders are discussed in one place, tracked in another, adjusted informally, and remembered manually. Over time, that creates friction across the entire procurement cycle.

Krētha was shaped as a product that sits between two extremes. On one side are informal tools like WhatsApp and spreadsheets, which are familiar but structurally weak. On the other are heavyweight ERP and CRM systems, which can be too complex, expensive, or rigid for smaller businesses. The opportunity was to design something more structured than the first, but much lighter and more accessible than the second.

India's B2B online marketplace is expected to become a US$200 billion opportunity by 2030, supported by broader digital adoption, stronger infrastructure, and policy support around e-commerce and digital business systems. That broader shift created the right conditions for procurement tools that are operationally useful, mobile-friendly, and designed for the way smaller businesses actually work.Source: IBEF — India E-commerce Industry Overview

Design Position

More structured than informal tools. Much lighter than enterprise software. Built for the way smaller businesses actually work.

The Problem Space

Procurement here is rarely
a single-screen task

Retailers often need to compare suppliers, repeat known purchases, coordinate with staff, edit quantities, confirm changes, and keep operational continuity even when stock decisions are distributed across people and time. For many retailers, placing an order means comparing suppliers, checking stock rhythm, adjusting quantities, coordinating with internal staff, confirming changes, and making sure the final order still reflects business reality.

Design framing

Fragmentation is not the absence of a tool — it is what happens when each part of the process uses a different one, with no shared record of what was agreed.

Three core challenges

01
Fragmented, multi-channel coordination

When that process happens through messaging threads, calls, spreadsheets, and memory, the result is slower ordering, avoidable errors, poor visibility, and weak auditability.

02
Repetition without infrastructure

Retailers rarely buy in isolated moments. They reorder familiar products in recurring cycles shaped by seasonality, storage limits, sales velocity, cash flow, and supplier availability. Without a system that supports repeat purchasing well, every order starts to feel like it is being rebuilt from scratch — increasing cognitive load and making procurement harder to manage across multiple people or locations.

03
Organisational complexity at small scale

B2B procurement is not just a buyer action; it is an organisational process. Accounts need to represent businesses, not only individuals. That introduces questions around roles, permissions, pricing, collaboration, approvals, and accountability. The design challenge was to support that complexity without overwhelming smaller operators who still need speed, clarity, and affordability.

Research & Discovery

Operational behaviour over abstract personas

The discovery phase focused less on abstract personas and more on operational behavior. The central question was not simply what interface should come next, but how procurement actually happens inside working businesses and where the current process starts to break down.

That shift changed the nature of the research. Instead of focusing primarily on isolated user preferences, the work centered on workflows, dependencies, recurring actions, accountability, exception handling, and the relationship between procurement and broader business operations. The goal was to understand how inventory planning, supplier coordination, repeat ordering, and internal collaboration connect in real life.

The market context reinforced the relevance of this problem. India's B2B online marketplace is projected to reach US$200 billion by 2030, supported by digital infrastructure growth and broader e-commerce momentum. At the same time, adoption among MSMEs still depends on products being simple, trustworthy, and practical rather than enterprise-heavy. That made it clear that Krētha should not imitate complex enterprise systems. It needed to fit naturally into the operating patterns of smaller businesses. Source: IBEF — India E-commerce Industry Overview

The available product documentation reinforced the same structural assumptions. Krētha was explicitly designed for retailers, wholesalers, and manufacturers, which confirmed that the platform needed to support multiple business actors rather than a simple buyer-seller interaction model. The documentation also pointed to business accounts, organization details, and platform-based information sharing, validating the need for role-aware workflows and organization-level structures rather than consumer-style assumptions.

This phase produced workflow maps, role relationships, and decision paths for key procurement events such as draft order creation, supplier review, changes, confirmations, and post-order follow-up. It also surfaced practical constraints that would directly shape the design quality of the MVP, including backend dependencies, phased development, permission complexity, and the need to keep the early product understandable without compromising its long-term integrity.

Finding 01
Procurement is collaborative

Even when one person appears to place the order, decisions are often distributed across people and time — the platform needed to support that coordination, not assume a single decision-maker.

Finding 02
Repeat ordering is critical

Reordering familiar SKUs in cycles is one of the strongest behavioural patterns in retail. It deserves first-class feature design, not an afterthought — because it directly affects operational efficiency.

Finding 03
Business users need control

Speed matters for high-volume ordering, but only when it does not remove reviewability, auditability, or the ability to handle exceptions and incomplete states gracefully.

Target Audience

Designing for retailers, suppliers, and the networks around them

The platform needed to serve both sides of procurement while respecting the behaviours already common among small business users. Rather than designing for a single actor, Krētha had to accommodate distinct operational needs across the full supply network — from the kirana shop placing a weekly order to the regional distributor managing dozens of incoming purchase requests.

Primary
Retailers

Kirana shops, specialty stores, pharmacies, and apparel sellers that need simple order placement, supplier discovery, repeat purchasing, and transaction history — without a steep learning curve or high monthly costs.

Primary
Suppliers & Distributors

Regional wholesalers and FMCG distributors managing fragmented orders from multiple small retail clients, who need centralised order intake, clearer fulfilment visibility, and consistent communication.

Secondary
Agents & Networks

Agents, brokers, and SME associations that can facilitate platform adoption and support digital procurement at scale across their networks — acting as trusted intermediaries.

My Role

Product thinking, systems design, and live implementation support

The work on Krētha extended beyond interface design into product thinking, systems design, and implementation support. The role included UX strategy, workflow definition, interaction design, information architecture, edge-case planning, and translating operational requirements into product flows that could be built iteratively in a live environment.

This was not a project where design happened in isolation and then moved downstream. Decisions were shaped through continuous collaboration with developers and business stakeholders. Feasibility, sequencing, backend constraints, permission logic, and phased rollout all influenced the way features were structured and prioritized.

Systems Thinking & Information Architecture

The platform had to behave like a connected system, not a menu of tools

One of the most important realizations in the project was that Krētha could not be treated as a collection of isolated features. Order placement, purchase history, saved lists, account roles, subscriptions, and supplier interaction all influence one another. The information architecture therefore had to function as a connected system rather than a menu of independent tools.

This systems view changed the way product decisions were made. An order was no longer treated as a simple transaction record, but as part of a larger lifecycle involving procurement intent, internal coordination, supplier response, revisions, and fulfillment visibility. In the same way, a purchase list was not just a saved cart. It became a planning object connected to recurring buying behavior, stock management, and business efficiency.

The navigation and hierarchy were shaped by the same logic. The design needed to make relationships clear: who belongs to which organization, who is allowed to prepare or approve an order, which lists are personal or shared, what each subscription tier unlocks, and how order states change based on business logic. Clarity in these relationships became as important as visual simplicity.

Layer 01
Business Entities

Retailers, wholesalers, and manufacturers interact on the platform as distinct business actors — each with different ordering behaviours, data needs, and operational responsibilities.

Layer 02
Organisational Structure

Accounts are tied to organisation details, multi-user access, and role-specific permissions. The system had to support real business identity — not just individual users — from early in the architecture.

Layer 03
Operational Workflows

Browsing, ordering, editing, approving, fulfilling, and repeating purchases form a lifecycle — not a series of isolated screens. Design decisions had to hold across the full arc of a procurement event.

Designing the Core Flows

Three systems that define the platform

These are the primary procurement systems designed — each reflecting a distinct aspect of how businesses buy, plan, and coordinate within a structured operational environment. Each flow had to support both the ideal path and the realistic exceptions that define live operational work.

01
Core Flow
Order Flow

The order flow became the most important systems problem in the product. In consumer commerce, checkout is often designed around compression and speed. In Krētha, speed still mattered, but it had to coexist with reviewability, supplier coordination, order accuracy, and the ability to manage operational exceptions without undermining trust.

The experience evolved around a multi-step procurement journey rather than a single compressed checkout. A key design question was how much information should be visible upfront and how much should be progressively disclosed. Too much information created fatigue, especially for high-volume orders. Too little information hid the implications of supplier dependency, edits, and fulfillment conditions.

Wireframe 01 Order entry & line items
Wireframe 02 Review & submission

The final direction prioritized a structured sequence: build the order through clear line items and quantity controls, preserve visibility into supplier and business context, support review before submission, create room for edits or approval logic, and keep fulfillment status understandable after the order is placed.

A major design priority was reducing friction without oversimplifying the process. Procurement tasks are often repetitive and cognitively heavy, so the interface needed bulk actions, fast quantity changes, and predictable review states. But it also needed to respect the reality that B2B orders are often subject to confirmation, revision, and controlled change management rather than instant final submission.

Edge cases were especially important here. Draft orders, supplier-side changes, partially edited orders, role-restricted approvals, and users who can prepare but not finalize an order all shaped the flow. Designing for those cases made the product feel credible inside a real operating environment rather than merely polished in a static prototype.

02
Core Flow
Purchase Lists

Purchase Lists emerged from one of the clearest behavioral patterns in the research: repeat ordering is central to retail procurement. Retailers often buy the same products repeatedly, but timing, quantity, and supplier choice change based on stock, sales, and cash position. Reconstructing those orders manually every time is inefficient and unnecessary.

The feature was therefore designed as an operational shortcut rather than a convenience add-on. Its purpose was to reduce repeat effort, support bulk buying behavior, and help users initiate routine procurement with fewer clicks and less dependence on memory.

Several decisions shaped the feature. Lists were treated as reusable procurement assets rather than temporary carts. Naming and organizing lists needed to reflect real business behavior, whether by supplier, category, or seasonal need. Bulk selection and list-to-order transitions had to be easy, while still allowing users to adapt saved patterns to current inventory needs.

This also introduced a planning dimension. A list should support speed, but it should not imply that procurement is static. Quantities change, availability changes, and business priorities change. That made Purchase Lists a bridge between planning and execution rather than just a faster path to checkout.

Design decisions
Reusable assets, not carts

Lists are persistent procurement objects — not temporary selections. They can be named, organised by category or supplier, and maintained across purchasing cycles.

Bulk selection and fast transitions

Users can select items in bulk and convert a list to an order with minimal rework — reducing the gap between planning intent and procurement action.

Flexible for live inventory

Lists adapt to live conditions: quantities can be adjusted, items can be swapped, and the structure leaves room for the inventory realities that repeat ordering must accommodate.

Supports planning, not just execution

Because lists persist over time, they carry planning context — seasonal patterns, supplier preferences, stock cycles — making them useful reference points beyond the immediate order.

03
Core Flow
Subscription & Organisation Structure

Subscription and organization design introduced another layer of complexity because business model logic had to coexist with usability. Krētha was not being designed for single operators alone. The documentation showed that accounts included organization details and that the platform facilitated information exchange across multiple business actor types, making it necessary to think early about permissions, scale, and multi-user structures.

The challenge was to create a structure that could support growth without making smaller businesses feel administratively burdened or priced out. That led to a tiered way of thinking: lightweight access for smaller teams, clearer value as organizations expand, and role-based capabilities that match real business responsibilities.

This area required especially close alignment between UX, business strategy, and engineering. Interface decisions such as who can invite team members, how shared information behaves, and what each plan unlocks have downstream implications for backend models, access control, support complexity, and long-term scalability.

Design considerations
Accounts reflect real business identity

Account creation establishes an organisational context from the start — not just an individual profile. This shapes how access, data sharing, and platform behaviour work across the whole team.

Access follows responsibility

Roles for order preparation, approval, and administration mean users have the access their work requires — without every team member needing to manage functions that belong elsewhere.

Tiers grow with the organisation

Subscription tiers were designed to expand naturally — lightweight for smaller teams getting started, with more coordination and administration capabilities becoming available as the business scales.

Coordination without confusion

Multi-user access patterns needed to make clear what is personal versus shared, who can bring in teammates, and how organisation-wide data behaves — enabling collaboration without removing accountability.

How the Work Happened

Designing in parallel, not ahead

A defining part of the project was designing in parallel with development rather than handing off completed concepts in isolation. Because Krētha is a live product, the work naturally involved trade-offs between ideal user flows and practical implementation sequencing.

That collaboration influenced the product in several ways. Feasibility checks helped validate whether interaction patterns matched the available data models and timelines. Constraint-based iteration helped simplify or restructure flows when dependencies or states were not yet practical to build. Clear documentation of behaviors, conditions, and edge cases reduced ambiguity during implementation. As features moved into development, new friction points also created opportunities to refine not just the interface, but the product model itself.

This was especially important in areas such as order-state handling, role restrictions, and organization-based permissions, where UX decisions and system architecture are tightly connected. Treating developers as product collaborators rather than downstream executors improved the quality and realism of the work.

It also made the project more honest. Instead of presenting design as a polished final artifact, Krētha reflects the actual nature of product work: proposing, testing, adapting, negotiating constraints, and improving the system through iteration.

Principle
Feasibility alignment

Checking early whether an interaction idea was grounded in available data structures and timelines meant fewer false starts — and helped the team commit to directions that were buildable, not just desirable.

Principle
Constraint-based iteration

When certain states, rules, or backend dependencies were not yet practical to build, constraints became a design tool rather than a blocker — sharpening what the flow actually needed to be rather than weakening the process.

Principle
Handoff clarity

Documenting behaviors, conditions, and edge cases with enough specificity meant implementation stayed close to intent — and when friction surfaced during build, it opened up conversations about improving the product model, not just the interface.

Design System & Scalability

Consistency as a product property, not a visual one

As the platform expanded, consistency became less about visual polish and more about operational predictability. Reusable components were necessary not only to speed up design and development, but to make different workflows feel familiar and trustworthy.

The system design focused on reusable form and table patterns for dense business workflows, shared interaction logic for status and permissions, modular structures for procurement and account-related features, and standardized patterns for bulk actions, decision states, alerts, and confirmations.

This consistency matters because complex products become difficult to trust when each section behaves differently. A coherent design system lowers the learning curve, supports future expansion, and helps the platform move from an early-stage product toward a more mature operational environment.

Scalability also extended beyond components. Navigation, entity relationships, and content density all needed to support the current product while leaving room for future supplier workflows, richer fulfillment logic, larger organizations, and more advanced business administration features.

Reflection & Learnings

Strong product design makes systems understandable, not screens look finished

Krētha reinforced that strong product design is often less about making screens look finished and more about making systems understandable. The most valuable work in the project came from clarifying relationships, sequencing actions carefully, surfacing the right information at the right time, and designing for the messiness of real operational behavior.

The project also strengthened the importance of designing with implementation in mind. Technical constraints did not reduce the quality of the process; they sharpened it. They forced clearer prioritization, more explicit assumptions, and more durable decisions about what the product needed to be now versus what it could become later.

Several lessons became especially clear. Real procurement flows need exception handling as much as ideal-path design. Repeat behavior deserves first-class support because it directly shapes efficiency. Multi-user business products need early systems thinking around identity, permission, and coordination. And scalability is often an information architecture problem before it becomes a visual design problem.

If the next phase were expanded further, the strongest opportunities would likely be deeper fulfillment visibility, stronger inventory-linked planning, more advanced approval logic, and sharper team administration tools. Those areas would continue strengthening the relationship between procurement, operations, and business coordination.

Current Status & Next Steps

An active product, not a closed artifact

Krētha is best understood as an active product in development rather than a closed portfolio artifact. Its current state is shaped by live constraints, iterative delivery, and a platform vision that continues to evolve around retailer, wholesaler, and manufacturer workflows.

The next phase would build on the foundations that are already in place. That includes expanding visibility across post-order states, strengthening shared workflows for teams and organizations, refining Purchase Lists into stronger planning tools, and evolving subscription tiers around collaboration depth, role management, and business scale.

What makes Krētha strategically meaningful is not simply that it enables ordering. The product reframes procurement as a system of coordinated business actions. That makes it a strong example of systems-focused UX design: work that supports operations, handles complexity, and grows through collaboration rather than surface-level polish.
