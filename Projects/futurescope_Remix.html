Project Brief

Enhancing
Retirement Strategies

Retirement planning in the UK has a perception problem. It is widely understood to be important, yet consistently avoided. The aim of this project was to understand why — and to design a service that could bridge the gap between awareness and action.

The starting point was a simple observation: most planning tools treat retirement as a rational calculation. FutureScope treated it as a behavior-change challenge. That shift changed everything about the design.

People in their mid-30s to 50s begin recognizing the importance of financial planning but often lack the tools and the emotional readiness to act. Existing solutions are either too complex, overly technical, or fail to meet users where they actually are.

Why this problem matters

Three contributing factors shaped the design challenge.

The absence of early financial planning creates compounding insecurity later in life — a problem that grows quietly until it cannot be ignored.

Behavioral biases, particularly present bias, make it cognitively difficult for people to connect with their future selves. When you cannot picture who you are planning for, it is hard to feel motivated to plan.

Many people do not seek financial advice at all — not because it is unavailable, but because the process feels intimidating, impersonal, or untrustworthy.

6 key barriers to retirement planning in the UK

The 2021 UK Adult Financial Wellbeing Survey identified six distinct barriers that prevent people from engaging meaningfully with retirement planning: psychological (present bias), cultural (avoidance of money conversations), awareness (lack of pension literacy), digital exclusion (unequal access to planning tools), trust (scepticism toward financial institutions), and socio-economic (structural inequality in contributions and outcomes).

FutureScope was designed to address several of these barriers simultaneously — not through information alone, but through a guided experience that starts with reflection rather than calculation.

Target Audience Analysis

Who we designed for

Primary Users
Adults aged 35–50

These are individuals who have begun to recognize the importance of long-term financial planning but struggle to translate that awareness into action. They are often managing competing priorities — housing, family, travel, career progression — while retirement remains abstract and emotionally distant. They do not need more data. They need a way in.

Secondary Users
Younger & older individuals

Younger individuals interested in financial independence and the FIRE(Financial Independence Retiring Early) community, and older individuals already in the process of managing accumulated savings, represent adjacent audiences who could benefit from the same underlying service philosophy.

Objective

Understand user behaviors, attitudes, and pain points around retirement planning.

Approach

A survey was conducted with 12 participants (aged 25–50), recruited via Reddit and Discord. Four in-depth interviews followed, exploring user motivations and anxieties in greater depth. Both methods were anchored around a Buzzfeed-style quiz used as a boundary object — a research technique drawn from Frith et al. (2005) and Bagnoli (2009) — which used unlabelled imagery to help participants describe their future without the constraints of financial language.

At the end of each quiz, participants were shown an estimate of how much money they would need to support their chosen lifestyle, calculated using the HSBC Retirement Calculator. This moment consistently prompted surprise, reflection, and more grounded conversation about what retirement planning actually means.

"I'm nowhere near saving that amount, but this result makes me feel less anxious. It's about the sum I've been aiming for."

— Interview participant

The unlabelled format was deliberate. Removing category labels reduced researcher-imposed bias and allowed participants to respond more expressively. Several asked to select more than one option — a signal that lifestyle expectations resist clean categorisation and that any good design should reflect that nuance.

Key survey findings

The only measurable difference between age groups was expected retirement duration: those under 30 anticipated retiring for 10–15 years, while those over 30 expected 15–20 years. In all other respects, confusion and avoidance were consistent across age groups.

Around 50% of respondents said retirement did not factor into major purchasing decisions. Most described a desired retirement involving travel, hobbies, and downtime — but had no savings strategy aligned with those aspirations.

The Core Insight

Regardless of age, people are aimless about retirement planning not because they lack information, but because they lack imagination about their future selves.

Summarizing Research / Takeaways

What was the
take away from the research?

The research surfaced a clear pattern: people knew retirement planning mattered, but most struggled to begin. The gap between awareness and action — between knowing the future is coming and being able to picture it — became the heart of the problem we set out to solve. One participant captured it directly:

Problem Statement

"I am a 35-year-old woman living and working in the UK. I am trying to develop a savings plan for retirement, but I am struggling to do so because I cannot imagine what my needs will be that far in the future. This has caused me to feel overwhelmed and ill-prepared for my retirement."

The research revealed that one of the biggest barriers to retirement planning is not a lack of awareness, but a lack of imagination. Users found it difficult to translate an ideal future lifestyle into a concrete savings strategy. Without a clear picture of what retirement might look like, long-term planning remained vague and emotionally disconnected.

Another important insight was that anxiety shapes behavior. Participants often felt uneasy when confronted with savings estimates and did not know where to begin. In many cases, existing financial tools increased that anxiety because they focused on numbers before helping users understand their goals.

The research also showed that personalized support matters. Users responded more positively to tailored advice, guided conversations, and visible progress tracking than to generic financial information. That suggested trust and motivation grow when planning feels personal, human, and relevant.

Three ideas became central to the concept

  • 01Users need help visualizing their future in order to make realistic financial decisions.
  • 02Financial planning should feel engaging and jargon-free in order to reduce anxiety.
  • 03Personalized guidance increases trust, confidence, and motivation.
Hypothesis

If we provide an interactive, personalized financial planning experience, users will feel more confident and motivated to take actionable steps toward their retirement goals.

Solution

Access to financial information is not the barrier — emotional accessibility is

The research pointed consistently toward one conclusion: access to financial information is not the barrier. Emotional accessibility is. FutureScope was therefore designed not as a calculator, but as a guided experience — one that begins with self-reflection, builds confidence gradually, and keeps users engaged over time.

01
Onboarding quiz and personalized profiles

Users begin with a guided questionnaire that helps them define their aspirations, habits, and expectations for later life. Drawing directly from the Buzzfeed boundary object used in research, the quiz uses imagery and personality-based prompts rather than financial inputs. The goal is to help users articulate the life they want before confronting the numbers required to get there.

Each quiz result maps to a lifestyle profile — such as The Comfortable Life or The Adventure Profile — which becomes the foundation for everything that follows.

02
Expert consultation

After completing the quiz, users are matched with a financial advisor who reviews their profile and translates it into practical, personalized recommendations. This component directly addressed the clearest finding from the research: generic advice does not move people to act. Personalized guidance does.

The consultation also allows users to plan for scenarios in which their aspirational goals may not be immediately achievable — setting realistic expectations alongside motivating targets.

03
Progress tracking and insights, framed as a horoscope

This is where FutureScope's most distinctive design decision lives.

Most financial apps present progress as a dashboard: a set of numbers, charts, and completion percentages waiting to be interpreted. The problem is that interpretation requires motivation, and motivation requires engagement — and engagement is exactly what users in financial anxiety tend to withhold. A dashboard you dread opening stops working the moment you close it.

The horoscope offered a different model entirely. People read their horoscope not because they are obligated to, but because it speaks directly to them, arrives on its own schedule, and frames their situation in language that feels personal rather than clinical. It is low-pressure. It is recurring. It rewards a few seconds of attention without demanding more. And crucially — it tells a story about the future, not just a report on the present.

FutureScope borrowed that structure and applied it to financial progress. Rather than asking users to log in and interpret their own data, the app delivers a personalised weekly forecast. Each update is framed around the user's specific lifestyle profile and written in the voice of the oracle — warm, slightly mystical, but grounded in real numbers underneath. The data is unchanged. The feeling is entirely different.

Dashboard register

"You overspent this week."

Horoscope register

"Mercury warns of misalignment."

Five recurring card types anchor the experience, each serving a distinct emotional function:

I
The Forecast

Savings forecast. Confirms momentum and projects forward. Gives users a reason to feel good about what they are already doing.

II
The Warning

Spending alert. Surfaces a drift without accusation. Mercury warns of misalignment lands differently than You overspent this week.

III
The Triumph

Milestone celebration. Marks progress in real-world terms — six weeks of your retirement lifestyle rather than 34% of goal reached.

IV
The Opportunity

Opportunity insight. A nudge toward a specific, achievable action. Personalised to the user's profile, never generic.

V
The Sage

Weekly wisdom. A single piece of financial education, delivered at low stakes. Builds literacy gradually, without overwhelm.

The horoscope framing did something no dashboard feature could: it gave users a reason to look forward to hearing about their finances. The emotional register shifted from dread to curiosity. That shift is not cosmetic — it is the mechanism through which a passive user becomes an engaged one.

Progress is further made tangible through real-world equivalences throughout the app. Savings milestones are expressed as weeks of retirement funded, not raw figures. Each milestone earns a named tarot-style card — The Tower, The Moon, The World — turning an abstract accumulation into a collection of achievements with weight and narrative. The language of the mystic, deliberately, in service of the very practical goal of keeping someone saving for thirty years.
