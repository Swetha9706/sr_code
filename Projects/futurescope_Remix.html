Project Brief

Enhancing
Retirement Strategies

Retirement planning in the UK has a perception problem. It is widely understood to be important, yet consistently avoided. The aim of this project was to understand why — and to design a service that could bridge the gap between awareness and action.

The starting point was a simple observation: most planning tools treat retirement as a rational calculation. FutureScope treated it as a behavior-change challenge. That shift changed everything about the design.

People in their mid-30s to 50s begin recognizing the importance of financial planning but often lack the tools and the emotional readiness to act. Existing solutions are either too complex, overly technical, or fail to meet users where they actually are.

Why this problem matters

Three contributing factors shaped the design challenge.

The absence of early financial planning creates compounding insecurity later in life — a problem that grows quietly until it cannot be ignored.

Behavioral biases, particularly present bias, make it cognitively difficult for people to connect with their future selves. When you cannot picture who you are planning for, it is hard to feel motivated to plan.

Many people do not seek financial advice at all — not because it is unavailable, but because the process feels intimidating, impersonal, or untrustworthy.

Six key barriers to retirement planning in the UK

The 2021 UK Adult Financial Wellbeing Survey identified six distinct barriers that prevent people from engaging meaningfully with retirement planning: psychological (present bias), cultural (avoidance of money conversations), awareness (lack of pension literacy), digital exclusion (unequal access to planning tools), trust (scepticism toward financial institutions), and socio-economic (structural inequality in contributions and outcomes).

FutureScope was designed to address several of these barriers simultaneously — not through information alone, but through a guided experience that starts with reflection rather than calculation.

Solution

FutureScope is designed to simplify and personalize retirement planning through three core features

We developed a high-level concept for a personalised retirement planning service, including a quiz, a financial consultation meeting, and routine financial updates. This service would be virtual, with monthly updates similar to a daily horoscope — designed to feel approachable and ease the anxiety surrounding future financial planning.

01
Onboarding Quiz & Personalized Profiles

Users complete a guided questionnaire to identify their lifestyle goals and savings needs. Drawing on our Buzzfeed boundary object research, the quiz uses imagery and personality indicators to make the process feel engaging rather than intimidating.

02
Expert Consultation

A financial advisor provides tailored recommendations based on the user's profile. This directly addresses our core research finding: personalized guidance is far more effective than generic advice at motivating real action.

03
Progress Tracking & Insights

A mobile-first interface helps users monitor their savings progress with engaging visualizations and periodic updates — delivered like a monthly horoscope to maintain consistent engagement without creating additional anxiety.

Research Question

"How can we support individuals in the UK between the ages of 35–50 to envision the realities of their retirement and determine what they will need to sustain a certain lifestyle?"

Research Methods

How we discovered the problem

01
Method
Surveys & Interviews
Objective

Understand user behaviors, attitudes, and pain points regarding retirement planning.

Approach

Conducted a survey (12 participants, aged 25–50) via Reddit & Discord. Conducted 4 in-depth interviews to explore user motivations and challenges. Used a Buzzfeed-style quiz as a boundary object to help participants visualize their future lifestyles.

We created a boundary object using a custom Buzzfeed quiz to help initiate a discussion about lifestyle expectations in retirement. The quiz consisted of multiple-choice questions with captioned images representing response options.

The quiz included questions on daily activities and lifestyle choices. Each response included a set of unlabelled images — the rationale was to reduce researcher bias and allow participants to be more expressive in their answers.

Imagery is an effective interview tool to elicit deeper introspection and establish a rapport with the interviewee while giving the participant more control in picking imagery that is more meaningful to them. — (Frith et al., 2005; Bagnoli, 2009)

The survey was posted on Reddit and Discord, receiving 12 responses. Of the 12, 11 indicated they reside in the UK. Around 35% of respondents were between 25–30 years old, and 63% were between 35–45. Most respondents were male (90%), and nearly half worked in the tech sector.

A key difference between age groups was expected retirement length. Those under 30 anticipated retiring for 10–15 years, while those over 30 expected 15–20 years — possibly due to rising living costs and fewer projected earning years.

💡 Key takeaway: Regardless of age, people are somewhat aimless about their retirement planning and struggle to envision their realistic needs. There was no clear distinction between age groups when it came to retirement planning.

We conducted four in-depth interviews with individuals aged 27–40 using the Buzzfeed quiz as a boundary object. At the end of each quiz, participants were presented with a result estimating how much money they would need to maintain their desired lifestyle. Estimates were based on the HSBC Retirement Calculator.

"I'm nowhere near saving that amount, but this result makes me feel less anxious. It's about the sum I've been aiming for."

3 participants expressed interest in a more luxury lifestyle but were surprised by the savings necessary to sustain it. Using a boundary object allowed for more introspection into personality types through prompted questions such as:

"Would you consider yourself an optimist or realist?"

For one participant who expressed a more optimistic life perspective:

"I don't consider myself someone with a luxury lifestyle, but I think it's good to have ambitious goals."

Surveys and research showed that access to financial planning resources alone wasn't enough. Summarizing Research / Takeaways

What was the
take away from the research?

The research surfaced a clear pattern: people knew retirement planning mattered, but most struggled to begin. The gap between awareness and action — between knowing the future is coming and being able to picture it — became the heart of the problem we set out to solve. One participant captured it directly:

Problem Statement

"I am a 35-year-old woman living and working in the UK. I am trying to develop a savings plan for retirement, but I am struggling to do so because I cannot imagine what my needs will be that far in the future. This has caused me to feel overwhelmed and ill-prepared for my retirement."

The research revealed that one of the biggest barriers to retirement planning is not a lack of awareness, but a lack of imagination. Users found it difficult to translate an ideal future lifestyle into a concrete savings strategy. Without a clear picture of what retirement might look like, long-term planning remained vague and emotionally disconnected.

Another important insight was that anxiety shapes behavior. Participants often felt uneasy when confronted with savings estimates and did not know where to begin. In many cases, existing financial tools increased that anxiety because they focused on numbers before helping users understand their goals.

The research also showed that personalized support matters. Users responded more positively to tailored advice, guided conversations, and visible progress tracking than to generic financial information. That suggested trust and motivation grow when planning feels personal, human, and relevant.

Three ideas became central to the concept

  • 01Users need help visualizing their future in order to make realistic financial decisions.
  • 02Financial planning should feel engaging and jargon-free in order to reduce anxiety.
  • 03Personalized guidance increases trust, confidence, and motivation.
Hypothesis

If we provide an interactive, personalized financial planning experience, users will feel more confident and motivated to take actionable steps toward their retirement goals.
