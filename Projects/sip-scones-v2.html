01 Background

Designing for atmosphere, not just ordering

Afternoon tea is a social ritual shaped by tradition, pacing, and ambience. The brief called for a customisable food and drink ordering system for a restaurant tablet — but the real design challenge was subtler: how do you bring a deeply human, unhurried experience into a digital interface without disrupting it?

Early research showed that the digital system needed to feel thematically coherent with afternoon tea culture — deliberate, calm, and intuitive enough to require no instruction. Instead of entrance kiosks or personal phones, the concept places a tablet at each table so diners keep control of the order while the restaurant preserves its intimacy and mood.

"Technology should enhance the experience, not disrupt it."
— Guiding design principle
02 Design Principles

Three ideas that shaped every decision

Before any wireframes, three principles anchored the design — each one a direct response to what afternoon tea culture demands from a digital experience.

☕
Unhurried interaction
Every screen should feel calm and deliberate — no urgency cues, no countdown timers, no pressure. Diners browse at their own pace.
🧁
Thematic coherence
The interface metaphors — tea trays, teacup loyalty icons, layered tiers — mirror the physical ritual so the system feels familiar without instruction.
✦
Constrained delight
Working within LiveCode's limits forced every feature decision toward what users actually needed — simplicity became a design asset, not a compromise.
03 Screen Flow

The ordering journey was mapped in Figma as a clear sequence that encouraged exploration without overwhelming diners. Each step builds on the last — guiding users from welcome to payment without backtracking or confusion.

04 Prototype Testing

Layout mattered more than expected

Two interface concepts were tested using paper prototypes and a Wizard of Oz method — manually simulating interactions while two participants used the system. The approach surfaced meaningful differences in perception and navigation despite a small sample.

Note: Small-scale guerrilla testing with 2 participants, suited to formative concept validation rather than statistical measurement.

05 User Testing

The full-tier view felt more intuitive and satisfying

The visual metaphor became one of the strongest design elements — and testing confirmed it was the right call. Six findings shaped the next iteration.

Key finding
Strong concept, minimal friction
Both users completed their tasks with only minor issues. Participants praised the visual design, ordering flow, and thematic fit — the design language resonated clearly with the afternoon tea setting.
Gap identified
Switching menus felt rigid
Users wanted more fluid movement between food tiers and drinks to create better pairings. The fixed sequence felt limiting for those who wanted to revisit earlier choices.
Accessibility note
Onboarding needed clearer cues
Participants asked for clearer instructions, especially for users whose first language was not English — a prompt for more inclusive copywriting and visual guidance throughout.
Loyalty screen
Contextual guidance, not exploration
One user needed the Help button to understand the loyalty screen — validating the need for built-in contextual guidance rather than relying on users to explore their way to understanding.

06 Evaluation

Constraints shaped the product as much as ideas did

LiveCode made it possible to create interactive visual components without heavy coding, but it also introduced real limits — nested controls, no persistent data recall, restricted undo. These weren't weaknesses. They sharpened prioritisation and pushed the design toward clarity.

Working within limits forced better decisions about what actually mattered to the experience. The result was a prototype that felt focused and purposeful — not despite its constraints, but because of them.

07 If I Built This Again

What testing revealed I'd change first

Testing with real users surfaced gaps I wouldn't have caught alone. These are the six changes I'd prioritise in a second iteration, ordered by user impact.

01
Memory and navigation — Use stored variables to support back buttons and order revisions without losing progress. The most-requested change from testing.
02
Smarter dialogs — Replace default LiveCode dialog boxes with styled modals that match the interface aesthetic and feel part of the experience.
03
Visual checkout summary — Show the full tea tray and drink selections before payment. A moment for diners to appreciate what they've built — and catch any mistakes.
04
Checkout validation — Prevent users from proceeding without at least one selection. A simple guard that avoids empty-order confusion.
05
Real sign-up flow — Move beyond predefined personas toward personalised accounts and long-term reward tracking for returning visitors.
06
Inclusive onboarding — More visual guidance from the first screen, designed for users whose first language may not be English.

"User testing turned Sip & Scones from a functional prototype into something more delightful and usable — confirming that the visual tray metaphor was the right design decision from the start."
