Sip & Scones

A tablet-based digital ordering system inspired by the elegance of English afternoon tea. Diners can customise a three-tiered tea tray, pair it with drinks, and track purchases through a visual loyalty program.

Tools
LiveCode and Figma.
Role
Research, user flows, interface prototyping, usability testing, and LiveCode implementation.
Brief
Design a customisable food or drink ordering system for a restaurant tablet interface.
Outcome
A functional ordering prototype with a tray menu, curated drinks, payment simulation, and loyalty rewards.
scroll

User Needs

Designing for atmosphere, not just ordering

Afternoon tea is a social ritual shaped by tradition, pacing, and ambience. Early research showed that the digital system needed to enhance the experience without interrupting its quiet elegance.

The challenge was to bring the warmth and personality of afternoon tea into a tablet interface — keeping interaction feeling deliberate and unhurried, while still being intuitive enough to require no instruction.

Technology should enhance the experience, not disrupt it.
— Design principle, Sip & Scones
FormatTablet-based, table-side ordering — no personal devices required
The Concept

A discreet table-side ordering experience

Instead of using entrance kiosks or personal phones, the concept places a tablet-based menu at each table. Diners keep control of the order while the restaurant preserves the intimacy and mood of afternoon tea.

Build a tea tray
Customise sandwiches, scones, and sweets through a visual three-tiered interface that mimics the physical experience of assembling an afternoon tea stand.
Pair drinks
Choose from a curated set of teas, coffees, and hot beverages — illustrated with icons and simplified radio buttons to reduce clutter.
Earn rewards
Track loyalty through a stack of teacups — a visual and thematically fitting metaphor, with four cups unlocking a 30% discount.
User Flow

A linear journey with visual richness

The ordering journey was mapped in Figma as a clear sequence that encouraged exploration without overwhelming diners. Each step builds on the last, guiding users from welcome to payment without backtracking or confusion.

Welcome → Tier Selection → Drinks → Login (Optional) → Loyalty → Confirmation → Payment
Start with calm context
A visually rich welcome screen introduces the experience and leads users into tray selection — setting the mood before any decisions are required.
Assemble the tray
Selections populate the virtual tray in real time, giving diners a sense of building their tea experience — the visual metaphor became one of the strongest design elements.
Confirm and pay
A clear summary, payment simulation, and optional order-again action complete the journey cleanly — no dead ends or ambiguity.
Key Features

Theme-led interactions that work within low-code constraints

The application aimed to feel delightful and themed while staying realistic to implement in LiveCode. Every feature decision balanced the elegance of the afternoon tea experience with what was genuinely buildable.

Tiered tray menu
Landscape orientation mimics a physical afternoon tea tray and uses labelled visual indicators for each food tier — sandwiches, scones, and sweets each occupy their own layer.
Drinks selection
Illustrative icons and radio buttons simplify choices while clear visual states reduce clutter — keeping the interface focused and calm throughout.
Loyalty program
Each completed order earns a teacup icon — four teacups unlock a 30% discount, turning a transactional mechanic into a thematically coherent ritual.
Payment simulation
A Pay Now button triggers a confirmation dialog that mimics digital transaction feedback — providing closure to the ordering experience.
Help access
A persistent Help button simulates calling a server — supporting users during moments of uncertainty without breaking the flow or requiring a restart.
Order reset
Cancel Order prompts confirmation and then resets the process — reducing confusion and giving users a safe exit without accidental data loss.
Prototyping & Testing

Layout mattered more than expected

The team tested two interface concepts using paper prototypes and a Wizard of Oz method — manually simulating interactions while participants used the system. This surface-level testing surfaced meaningful differences in how users perceived and navigated each layout.

Option 01
Full-tier view

Users could see all three tiers at once and review the meal as a whole — closer to the physical experience of looking at a tea stand.

Option 02
Single-tier focus

The interface zoomed into one tier at a time to reduce visual clutter — but users felt less in control of the overall order.

User Testing

The full-tier view felt more intuitive and satisfying

Users liked seeing the whole tray because it felt close to assembling a physical afternoon tea stand. The visual metaphor became one of the strongest design elements — and testing confirmed it was the right call over the single-tier approach.

Switching menus
Users wanted more fluid movement between food tiers and drinks to create better pairings — the fixed sequence felt slightly limiting for those who wanted to revisit earlier choices.
Clear onboarding
Participants asked for clearer instructions, especially for users whose first language was not English — a prompt for more inclusive copywriting and visual guidance.
Loyalty clarity
One user needed the Help button to understand the loyalty screen — validating the need for subtle, contextual guidance built into the interface rather than relying on exploration.
Strong concept
Participants praised the visual design, ordering flow, and thematic fit of the tiered layout — the design language resonated clearly with the afternoon tea setting.
Minimal friction
Both users completed their task with only minor issues — a strong signal that the core flow was logical and learnable even without prior instruction.
More flexibility
Future versions would support back navigation and easier order editing without requiring a full restart — the biggest functional gap identified through testing.
Evaluation

Designing with low-code tools shaped the product

LiveCode made it possible to create interactive visual components without heavy coding, but it also introduced constraints around nested controls, limited undo, and more ambitious features such as persistent login or memory-based data recall.

These constraints were not weaknesses — they sharpened prioritisation and pushed the design toward clarity and focus. Working within limits forced better decisions about what actually mattered to the experience.

What comes next

Smarter dialogs — Replace default dialog boxes with styled modal dialogs that match the interface aesthetic and feel part of the experience.

Memory and navigation — Use stored variables to support back buttons and order revisions without losing progress — the most-requested change from testing.

Real sign-up flow — Move beyond predefined personas toward personalised accounts and long-term reward tracking for returning visitors.

Visual summaries — Show the full tea tray and drink selections before checkout for clarity and delight — giving diners a moment to appreciate what they've built.

Checkout validation — Prevent users from continuing without at least one selection — a simple guard that avoids empty order confusion.

Refined responsiveness — Use testing feedback to make the experience clearer, smoother, and more flexible — especially around tier switching and mid-flow edits.

"User testing turned Sip & Scones from a functional prototype into something more delightful and usable — confirming that the visual tray metaphor was the right design decision from the start."

Final Thoughts

Tools shape the design process as much as ideas do

The project showed that designing with constraints — whether from code, budget, or time — forces clearer priorities and a more focused user experience. LiveCode's limitations pushed every decision toward what the user actually needed, rather than what was technically impressive.

Sip & Scones demonstrated that a strong conceptual metaphor — the physical tea tray translated to screen — can carry an entire product. When the interface feels familiar, users trust it faster and enjoy it more.
